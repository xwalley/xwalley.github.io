Shooting Live Music on Film

Photography:Profession and Pastime
I've worked as both a studio photographer and an event photographer. I've also studied photography as my second concentration, alongside painting and a double major in art history. Learning what commerical and conceptual photography can do brought me closer to experimenting with the medium, pointing out and embracing the strengths and flaws within it. Outside the demands of a client, I am able to reflect the medium in it's most unfiltered, raw expressions, particularily through film.

Film
I prefer shooting film over digital. Overall, I prefer a hands-on approach regarding my practice as an artist, as it gives me direct control over the medium, allowing me to commit to more precise and involved decisions. The type of film used, the manual loading of film, and the developing of film are conditions in which I can apply my artistry, followed by the editing process. I admire film for it's raw quailities; my work embraces dust blemishing the image, the staticy noise rippling the image and the vibrating streaks of out of focus subjects, pointing out the medium of photography in it's purest form - contrary to the work done in the industry.

Live Muisc
I have been attending punk and metal shows as far back as middle school, and have continued since. I loved going to see artists and bands perform live, because of their dynamic and lively performaces. The kinetic energy potent in the genre of punk and metal is what motivated me to document it through film. The characteristics associated to live music is something I sought to express through film. Though, photography heavily relies on lighting consitency. Given that the gigs I attended were often underground basement shows, whose only light sources are loose string lights, I was also motivated by a challenge.

The Challenge of Film and Live Music
Shooting live performances with film poses many challenges, allowing me the opportunity to experiment with the medium, exhausting photography's purest form. Lighting and exposure adjustment is the greatest challenge. Having a low shutter speed will ensure light passes through the lens, but the subjects will appear to be blurred, lacking form and clarity. Pushing the film's ISO on the camera or through development can also increase light sensitivity, but accumulates noise. These challenges allow me to find solutions in creatives means, allowing me to make artistic decisions in the process of shooting, developing, editing, and printing. As shown above, the three photographs exemplify the consquence of shooting film in an underground basement show. The result is a gritty, rough texture encompased by noise, followed by strays of dust ornamenting the composition. The grit and grim of the film's material is relative to the underground basement scene, grungy and aggressive.
